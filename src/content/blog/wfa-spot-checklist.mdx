Working from a café sounds romantic until you're hunting for a free outlet at 10am, your laptop is at 12%, and the only food option is a croissant that won't survive until noon.

I've been doing WFA long enough to know that a good spot isn't just about the vibe. There are a few things I will not compromise on, and a few more that I've slowly added to the list after being burned one too many times.

## The non-negotiables

### Power outlets — and enough of them

This is the first thing I look for when I walk in. If there are no outlets near the seating area, I'm already thinking about leaving. It doesn't matter how good the coffee is.

One outlet shared between three people is also a problem. Ideally I want one that's mine — or at least accessible enough that I'm not doing the awkward "can I just plug in?" conversation with a stranger every hour.

### Connectivity that actually works

WiFi quality varies wildly, and "we have WiFi" on a menu board means nothing. My test: load a YouTube video. If it buffers, the WiFi is probably not going to hold up through a video call.

When the WiFi is bad, I fall back on my phone's mobile data. This is why I also check for signal — specifically 4G LTE or 5G coverage — before committing to a spot. Some cafés are in basements or corners of malls where signal drops to nothing. I've learned to check before ordering.

### Real food — not just coffee

This is the one I feel most strongly about. If the menu only has coffee and cake, I'm out by 12:30 because I have to go find lunch somewhere else. That breaks the whole flow of the day.

My ideal spot has actual food: rice, noodles, sandwiches — something that could count as a proper meal. Bonus points if the food is good enough that I'd want to stay for dinner too. When I find a place like that, I tend to stay for six or seven hours without thinking twice.

## Things I've learned to care about

### The noise level at the right time

A café that's quiet in the morning can become a loud social scene by noon. I've started paying attention to *when* a place gets busy, not just whether it's quiet when I arrive.

For focused work — writing, coding, reading — I want it calm enough that I'm not fighting the background noise. For calls, I need a corner or a space where I'm not broadcasting to the whole room. Some places have a back area or a second floor that's noticeably quieter. I always look for that.

### Air conditioning that's actually cold

This might sound trivial but in this climate it isn't. A café with weak AC slowly becomes uncomfortable after an hour, and once I'm sweating I'm not productive. I notice this a lot in places that look nice from the outside but have opened too many windows or run the AC on some kind of eco setting.

Cold AC, good seating, no afternoon sun hitting my face directly. That combination keeps me comfortable for a full day.

### Seating that doesn't wreck your back

Café chairs are not designed for eight hours of sitting. I know this and I still get caught off guard. The spots I keep coming back to have chairs with actual back support, or at least tables at the right height so I'm not hunching over my laptop.

A couch sounds comfortable until you've tried to type on one for three hours. I avoid couches now.

### They don't rush you out

Some places have unwritten rules about how long you can sit on one order. You can feel it — staff hovering, the pressure to order again every hour. I stay away from those.

The places I return to have an implicit understanding that working customers are good customers. They don't mind if you're there all afternoon. Sometimes they'll even come top up your water without being asked.

## My quick check when I arrive

Before I sit down and unpack, I do a fast scan:

1. Outlet visible and accessible near the table?
2. Signal on my phone — at least 4G?
3. Does the menu have actual food, not just drinks?
4. AC feels cold enough?
5. Noise level I can work in?

If the answer to the first three is yes, I'll sit. The rest I can work around.

---

The perfect WFA spot is rare, but they exist. When you find one, you protect it a little. You don't tell too many people about it. You go back on a Tuesday when it's quiet. You tip well.

That's the secret, really — once you find a good spot, treat it well and it'll be there when you need it.
