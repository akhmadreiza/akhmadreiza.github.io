The CAP theorem, introduced by Eric Brewer in 2000 and later proven by Gilbert
and Lynch, states that a distributed data store can provide at most **two** of
the following three guarantees:

- **Consistency** — every read receives the most recent write (or an error)
- **Availability** — every request receives a non-error response (not necessarily the most recent write)
- **Partition tolerance** — the system continues to operate when network partitions split nodes into groups that can't communicate

## Why You Can't Have All Three

In any real distributed system, network partitions *will* happen. Nodes go
down, cables fail, and packets get dropped. Once a partition occurs, you face
a forced choice: do you return a potentially stale response (choose A), or do
you refuse to respond until you can guarantee consistency (choose C)?

This is why the real tradeoff in practice is **CP vs AP**, not about dropping
partition tolerance.

## CP Systems

A CP system sacrifices availability during a partition. When nodes can't
communicate, the system refuses requests that can't be safely answered.

**Examples:** HBase, Zookeeper, etcd, Consul.

Use when: you can't afford to serve stale data — financial transactions,
leader elections, configuration management.

## AP Systems

An AP system stays available during a partition, but may return stale data.
Nodes continue serving reads and accepting writes independently; they reconcile
conflicts after the partition heals.

**Examples:** Cassandra, CouchDB, DynamoDB.

Use when: availability matters more than strict consistency — shopping carts,
DNS, social media feeds.

## The Nuance: PACELC

CAP only describes behavior during partitions. PACELC extends it by asking:
what's the tradeoff *without* a partition (i.e., normally)?

Even under no partition, there's a latency/consistency tradeoff. Replicating a
write to all nodes before acknowledging is more consistent but slower. Most
systems let you tune this per-operation.

## What This Means When Designing Systems

1. **Decide your failure mode first.** What's worse: returning stale data, or
   returning no data?
2. **Tune at the operation level.** Cassandra's quorum reads give you
   consistency when you need it without making all reads expensive.
3. **Design for conflict resolution.** AP systems need a story for when
   diverged replicas reconcile — last-write-wins, vector clocks, CRDTs.
